```
The observational units are the individual participants within the classrooms who complete the survey and provide data about their music preferences and age. This includes the students and the teachers/staff.
```

## Part B - Survey Evaluation:

Identify and describe survey features:

```

When copy-pasted from the website (https://www23.statcan.gc.ca/imdb/p2SV.pl?Function=getSurvey&SDDS=4430), I have written the answer in quotes.

1. Sample type:
    "The sample is based on a stratified design employing probability sampling"
    --> The main type is probability sampling, with the sub-type being stratified. 
    --> "The stratification is done at the province/census metropolitan area (CMA) level"

2. Sample size:
    "A field sample of approximatively 50,000 units was used. Among them, about 40,000 invitation letters to the electronic questionnaire were sent to selected households across Canada. A completion of 24,000 questionnaires was expected."
    --> Desired size was 50,000; Actual letters sent were 40,000; Completion of 24,000 was expected. 
    --> Note that in this case the unit represents one representative individual (age 15+) from each selected household

3. Target population: 
    "The target population for the GSS Giving, volunteering and participating includes all persons 15 years of age and older living in the ten provinces of Canada. It excludes full-time (residing for more than six months) residents of institutions."

4. Sampling frame: 
    "This survey uses a frame that combines landline and cellular telephone numbers from the Census and various administrative sources with Statistics Canada's dwelling frame. Records on the frame are groups of one or several telephone numbers associated with the same address (or single telephone number in the case a link between a telephone number and an address could not be established). This sampling frame is used to obtain a better coverage of households with a telephone number."
    --> In other words, the frame includes households with telephone numbers.

5. Survey mode(s):
    "Data are collected directly from survey respondents either through an electronic questionnaire or through CATI (computer assisted telephone interviewing)."

6. Timeline:
    "Collection period: Every 5 years, from September to December"
    
7. Response rate:
    "The overall response rate is 41.9%."

8. Weights:
    "More precise estimates of the sampling variability of estimates can be produced with the bootstrap method using bootstrap weights that have been created for this survey."
    --> Bootstrap was weighted in a way to correct for some biases. For example:
        - "For the 2018 GSS, an additional adjustment was added where basic characteristics of non-responding households, such as income and household composition, were extracted from administrative sources and then used to model and adjust nonresponse."
        - " Survey estimates were adjusted (weighted) to represent all persons in the target population, including those not covered by the survey frame."

9. Data processing:
    "Processing used the SSPE set of generalized processing steps and utilities"
    "Edits were performed automatically and manually at various stages of processing at macro and micro levels. They included family, consistency and flow edits. Family relationships were checked to ensure the integrity of matrix data. A series of checks were done to ensure the consistency of survey data. An example was to check the respondent age against the respondent birth date. Flow edits were used to ensure respondents followed the correct path and fix off-path situations. Error detection was done through edits programmed into the CATI system."
    "All survey records were subjected to computer edits throughout the course of the interview. The CATI system principally edited the flow of the questionnaire and identified out of range values."
    "If the interviewer was unable to correctly resolve the detected errors, the interviewer bypassed the edit and forwarded the data to head office for resolution. All interviewer comments were reviewed and taken into account by head office editing."
    --> All this to say, there were both AUTOMATED and MANUAL checks at various steps of the process. At first, the CATI system edited the flow of the questionaire as it went, so there was some processing on the fly for the telephone interviews. If anything went wrong with that, the interviewer handled the discrepency or forwarded to head office. Regarding the electronic survey, looks like they checked various things to make sure the data was consistent, for example checking the age against the birth date. 

10. Cleaning, imputation, etc
    CLEANING
    --> This includes all the data processing steps above, e.g. error detection
    --> Imputation (described below) is also a way of cleaning the data before analysis
    --> Weighting helps adjust the data and correct for biases and complexities
    --> Validation measures, such as:
        "a. Analysis of changes over time;"
        "b. Verification of estimates through cross-tabulations;"
        "c. Confrontation with other similar sources of data."
    --> Cleaning the data by applying confidentiality rules

    IMPUTATION
    "Except in a few cases, all imputations were made using donor records selected through a score function."..."Where donor imputation could not be used, mean imputation among a pool of donors was used"..."Imputation was carried out in nine steps."..."Overall, a value for family income was obtained for 81.7% of households."..."Missing information for the other respondents was imputed."
    --> This contains most of the relevant information about imputation. Essentially, they did have to impute missing data, and they used a score system for this with donor records. The rate of data completion seems to be fairly high, though (e.g. 81.7% for family income), so perhaps bias as a result of imputation is minimal. 

11. Sources of error:
    "the results of the survey remain subject to both sampling and non-sampling error."
    SAMPLING
    "As the data are based on a sample of persons, they are subject to sampling error. That is, estimates based on a sample will vary from sample to sample, and typically they will be different from the results that would have been obtained from a complete census." --> bootstrapping helps mitigate this
    NON-SAMPLING
    "Common sources of these errors are imperfect coverage and non-response."
    --> Imperfect coverage means, for example, households without telephones. This could create a population bias if those without telephones are not random per se, but biased towards one demographic.
    --> Non-response means either households or individuals who didn't respond. This was adjusted for in weights. 
    "Other types of non-sampling errors can include response errors and processing errors."

12. Limitations, known biases, etc
    LIMITATIONS
    --> Sampling bias
    --> Imperfect coverage
    --> Self-report, such as when asked for volunteering hours.
    --> Missing data and the use of imputation
    BIASES
    --> Population may be skewed towards "households with telephones", which could reflect higher income levels or other socioeconomic factors ("housing composition" was mentioned). 
    --> Missing data also introduces some bias as it may skew towards some demographics over others
```
